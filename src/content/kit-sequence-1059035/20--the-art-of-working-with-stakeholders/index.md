As you're getting established in your role, I recommend you take an inventory of your stakeholders and the other leaders you work with.

Here are some tips I've learned over the years.

## Who are your stakeholders, and what do they care about?

Imagine your team. Who are the people that care about the work you're doing? These are your **stakeholders**.

I typically divide stakeholders into:

1.  **Cross-functional leaders**: these are leaders from other functions whose work depends on your team's work.
2.  **Higher ups**: people who are further up in the organization, and need good information from your team so they can do their job. They also have power, and often have a distorted view of the situation.

It's probably easier for you to empathize with cross-functional leaders than it is to empathize with higher ups. Higher-ups usually struggle to get good information. They hate to be surprised. And they need to be informed in order to do their job. So they are appreciative when their information needs are taken care of.

## The stakeholder exercise

Draw a picture of your team, and around it, add your stakeholders. Draw a little picture of each person, and write their name next to it. I have terrible art skills, so they end up looking like stick figures.

​

Next to each person, write a little thought bubble, and describe what that person cares about. You'll probably see things like:

-   I mostly care about delivery. I want to be notified of changes to project schedules. I want regular updates on projects. I care about new risks and concerns.
-   I need to know when new releases are made, so my team can be on the lookout for problems.
-   I need to know immediatley when incidents happen.
-   I'm responsible for the quality of the user experience, so I want to make sure design concerns are being taken seriously, and discussions about design are happening.

It's not important to be 100% accurate, or list everything the person cares about. But just spending a few moments thinking about their information needs will make you an **AMAZING PARTNER** to them.

For each, just spend a few minutes thinking about how you'll provide what they need.

## Do an initial round with all your stakeholders

If you haven't already done so, you should do a round of 1-1s with each of them. Set up meetings with all of them, and questions. Here are some things I ask:

-   What problems are you seeing in my team?
-   Are you getting what you need from me?
-   What's your experience working with my team? Anything you see room for improvement with?
-   Do you have any advice for me?
-   Can you tell me more about your role, and how you work with my team? I want to make sure I understand what your day to day life is like, and how you interface with my team. How is your role evaluated? What makes you look good or bad?

It's a superpower if you can understand the value other roles have, and what they need from you. Your team will better coordinate with other parts of the company, and thus be more valuable and effective.
