## Being a manager is a hard job

Before I became a manager, I thought I knew what management would be like. I had been a tech lead for several years, and managed contractors. I'd even hired people and managed them before. I was interested in management, but I had no idea how difficult it would be.

It was difficult. I remember thinking, "this is like starting over from zero!" All of my skills helped, but the skills I need for this role were completely different.

And it was much more stressful than my previous role. Suddenly, people's lives and happiness depended on me doing a good job. In my role as a software engineer, if I made a mistake, it might cause issues I would have to fix. In my role as a manager, I could make someone hate their job. I could make them unhappy. And if I didn't do my job well, a whole team of people could be affected.

Management is a difficult job.

## You'll need support

The first year of being a manager is a big transition. You'll need support, and you should look for help to grow your skills quickly. So today's homework is to find a couple of ways to support your learning and growth.

## Evaluate your environment.

One of your first steps is to evaluate the environment you're in. How much support are you going to get from your workplace. How much will your manager aid you in your growth?

### What is your company like?

Every company is different. Every company has its own flavor of problems. So as you're evaluating how the company will support you, keep in mind that your company may or may not be the best place to copy from. Every company has its dysfunctions. And there are tradeoffs to every way of managing.

My advice is to spend a few minutes thinking about your company. What is great about the company, and how is it different from other places you've worked? And where do you think it's lacking?

You don't have to be at the best company to become a great manager. But take an inventory of how the company can support you, and where it might not be the best place to copy from.

You might make a list with these sections, and add a few bullet points for each section:

1.  What are things I think this company is really good at?
2.  Where do I see problems at this company?
3.  What is the management culture of the company like? (You may not know yet) What do I think about that?
4.  What things might I NOT want to emulate?

### What is your manager like?

Similarly, take stock of your manager. Are they going to be someone that will mentor you? Where do you think they're strong as a manager, and where do you think they might be lacking.

Even managers that you may not like particularly can be people that can teach you a lot. I've learned a great deal from people I had difficulty with.

You might make a list with these sections, and add a few bullet points for each section:

1.  Where can I learn from my manager? What is she really good at?
2.  Where do I think my manager is weaker? What might I not want to emulate?
3.  What's my level of trust with my manager? If they recommend an area of growth, do I trust their advice?

If you feel you can learn a lot from your manager, and they're invested in your growth, that's a tremendous opportunity. But even with an absent manager, or someone you don't feel you can learn from, there are other avenues for growth.

## Choose a couple ways to support your learning and growth

Regardless of how you assessed your company and manager, you're going to need some support. So here's your homework:

Action: chose at least one of these steps to support your learning and growth.  
​

1.  **Find some local people to meet with periodically**. Set up periodic lunches or coffees. You can find people from your network, from [Leaddev](https://leaddev.com/meetups) meetups, or from LinkedIn. Ask around. People are hungry for this, so you may get a better response than you expect (I got about a 90% yes rate when I did this at one point).  
    ​
2.  **Form an "m-team" at your company**. M-teams are management support groups. The basic idea is to find 3-7 other managers who are interested in meeting weekly for an hour. Set up a weekly meeting. The agenda is "what's challenging this week?". You can facilitate. Go around the table, and when it's your turn, you talk a little about what you're struggling with. The other managers listen and you talk through how to address those challenges. Facilitation is super important, so make sure the best facilitator runs the meeting. It's important to set these up well, so nobody "shows off", and everyone is willing to be vulnerable. But they're a tremendously valuable way to learn from each other and accelerate your learning. I have a [guide for implementing m-teams](https://www.rubick.com/implementing-mini-m-support-groups/?utm_source=ck-support-network&utm_medium=link&utm_campaign=support-network).  
    ​
3.  **Ask someone to be your mentor**. One of the most valuable exercises you can do is to make an inventory of the people around you and who is good at what. If you see someone you can learn from, set up a time with them and ask them if they're willing to mentor you on that topic. Be clear about what you want to learn from them, and have a conversation about what the parameters of the mentorship engagement will look like. Most people will appreciate being asked. They may or may not be too busy to take it on. But mentors can be a powerful way to grow faster.  
    ​
4.  **Find a coach**. You can find people who are professional coaches. Your company may even contract with companies or people that provide coaching. Ask your People Ops / HR department if they support managers in this way. If they don't, you can still seek out professionals to help. Ask around in your network, and google for paid engineering management coaches. I recommend meeting with a couple of people before you decide which you want to work with. I've worked with an "executive coach" before, and it was one of the best things I did to progress my career. It can be worth spending your own money on. I do this, but encourage you to interview a few people. Here's my [guide to finding a good coach](https://www.rubick.com/executive-advisor/).  
    ​
5.  **Join the Rands Leadership Slack**. One of the largest online communities for engineering managers is the [Rands leadership Slack](https://randsinrepose.com/welcome-to-rands-leadership-slack/). It's a very large group, and has a lot of rooms, for every topic imaginable. It can be a good resource for learning about particular topics.  
    ​
6.  **Talk with PeopleOps/HR about management training**. Many, if not most, companies offer some sort of management training. This training can be of varying quality, but you almost always can learn from these courses. Most courses of these types will be on topics more focused on people management: delegation, running 1-1s, performance management, and the like. These are valuable topics! Take advantage of them whenever they are available. To find out about these, ask People Ops / HR if they offer any management trainings.
