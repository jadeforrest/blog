If the existing meeting structure is working for you, keep it. Otherwise, I wanted to share the meetings I typically will set up on a local team. And share a few I tend to avoid.

## ✅ Sprint or cycle kickoff

-   Participants: whole team.
-   The Product Manager presents a goal (or milestone) the team is working towards that is less than a month away (and gives context on longer term goals). They answer questions.
-   The Engineering Manager shows a draft project plan of work to be done to reach that milestone. This is a week by week list of things we would demo. Get feedback on that plan.
-   Either review a technical plan, or make plans to develop a technical plan for that milestone.
-   Review what we're planning to do for the first demo (end of the week). Problem-solve the work to be done to be able to demo that work. The team sets up their own follow-up meetings to work together on any outstanding problems.

## ✅ Problem-solving meetings

These are meetings the team sets up themselves to solve technical or coordination issues.

-   Participants: anybody who needs to or can help solve the problem.
-   It can be useful to set aside time for these meetings, right after standups for example.
-   Most of the topics are timely, and the meetings should only be today or tomorrow. Otherwise, you substantially increase the amount of time it takes to deliver software. Every day is something like a 5% decrease in velocity.

## ❌ Sprint estimation meetings

-   I generally find it wasteful to have a team have a meeting to estimate all the work. In many places, it will be necessary. A couple alternatives are: change the size of stories (if you're delivering 2-6 stories a sprint, you won't need to spend much time on story estimation). Or you can have standards for how large a story is, and count the number of stories. Or guess yourself (if it's safe to do so). Or estimate as a part of something else.

## ✅ Sprint or cycle demo

-   You can make this weekly or more frequent than the sprint. Worth doing if you are dealing with longer sprints, or there is more change than your sprint cycle length.
-   Participants: whole team, plus stakeholders
-   Have each person demo their work. It’s okay if it’s incomplete, or not pretty. Or demoing a test or command line.

## ✅ Sprint or cycle planning meeting

-   These are also called sprint grooming meetings.
-   Participants: product manager, you, possibly one or two others.
-   Plan out what you think should happen next sprint or cycle.
-   Go through outstanding bugs.

## ✅ Standups

-   Short check-ins to make sure people are communicating.
-   I typically find async standups to not work as effectively.
-   You might be able to flex the frequency of this, depending on how well the team is communicating.

## ✅ Retrospectives

-   Come up with a few action items each retrospective to improve the way the team operates.
-   Assign them to yourself as manager, or make a story to cover them, and schedule it for the next sprint.

## Or... you can have two meetings a week

An alternative approach you can take is to copy from [FAST agile](https://www.fastagile.io). Instead of having so many meetings, you have only one type of meeting, twice a week. This is a more experimental approach, but I think it's worth trying out. (I personally am looking for an opportunity to try this out)

## ✅ FAST meeting

Instead of having standups, demos, kickoffs, etc, you can combine everything into two meetings a week (or a meeting every two business days), each about 30 minutes each.

In this meeting, the team shows work they've completed, or work in progress. Most importantly, the Product Manager describes the current priorities and gives context. And then the team self-assembles into working groups to work against those priorities. People can move between working groups every FAST meeting.

The Product Manager and Engineering Manager will probably also have some preparation to do for the meetings. But overall, this seems like a lower overhead way of managing team meetings.
