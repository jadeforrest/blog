Let's turn our attention to people matters.

Over the next few posts, I'll share a few exercises you can do to better support your team members. We'll start with a performance review review:

## Performance review review exercise

Most companies have places where performance reviews are stored. And most of the time, as a manager, you have access to previous performance reviews. When you do, this is an exercise I often take when I join a team, or have the cycles to do it for a team I've been supporting for a while.

### What do you do?

1.  Read every performance review for each person on your team.
2.  For each review, write a one sentence summary of the advice the manager was making to your team member.
3.  Next to each one sentence summary, add brief commentary in brackets: Was the feedback helpful? Specific? Accurate? Biased? Something you need to investigate or think more about?

It will probably end up looking something like this:

_John Foster_:

-   I recommend you get better at communication \[not specific about what type of communication\]
-   Keep on doing what you're doing \[terrible advice\]
-   \[I couldn't make out what the manager was recommending\]
-   Make space for other people to speak in group setting \[rings true, specific, review didn't spell out why\]
-   You need to learn to listen as well as you speak, give space for others \[consistent with previous feedback, rings true\]

### Why is this worth doing?

-   You'll see patterns in the feedback people have received over time. Often a previous manager will have a take on it that will crystalize for you how to better support them.
-   You'll see abundant examples of good and bad reviews. And you'll see whether it's possible to distill the review down to something you can remember (one sentence). This will help you be better at giving them advice in the future. And it will help you write better reviews.
-   You'll also see what perceptions about the employee may be lingering in other parts of the organization. That can help you to be effective in countering any perceptions that are dated or unfair.

### Things to be careful about

There are dangers to reading previous reviews:

-   People learn, grow, and change. The advice from two years ago may no longer be pertinent if the person has grown in response to that advice.
-   Managers can be biased or wrong. Be especially vigilant around people who performed poorly in difficult circumstances. I can't tell you how often I've seen people from underrepresented groups be put into an impossible situation, and then be criticized for not making it magically better.

### What if you don't have previous reviews?

If you don't have access to previous performance reviews, you can do this the hard way. You can ask previous managers for the reviews. Or interview previous managers. I only recommend that if things aren't going well for that person. But we'll cover that in our next course.
