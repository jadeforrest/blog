## Assignment

If you haven't already, it's time to set up recurring one on ones ("1-1s") with your team.

Action: Set up 1-1s with all your team. By the end of the day today. 25 minutes each.

## Why do 1-1s?

As a manager, your job is shifting from doing work directly to doing work _through_ people. You will still have areas where you do work directly. But 1-1s are the primary way you grow the people on your team, and make sure you're aware of the problems on your team.

Having good information is more important now that you are a leader. You'll learn a lot more if you spend 1-1 time with your team.

## Who should you do 1-1s with?

Your team for now. You can do lateral 1-1s, but I'd try to restrict that to a couple of your most important working relationships (and we'll set that up later). It is possible to overuse 1-1s.

## How often should you do 1-1s?

I recommend weekly 1-1s if you can handle it. If you're feeling overwhelmed, it's fine to move to every other week 1-1s. If you have more than eight direct reports, start thinking about less frequent 1-1s.

## Should you bunch them up, or spread them out?

I've tried both, and I like to spread them out. But many people find that stacking up 1-1s on a particular day works for them.

## Tips for calendaring

-   Make the invite editable. Mention in the calendar invite that it is editable, and that they can move the invite if the time doesn't work for them.
-   Try to put the meeting next to lunches or other meetings, to minimize disruption to THEM.
-   Consider color-coding all your 1-1s a particular color.
-   Create a google doc (or equivalent) and share it with each 1-1 recipient. Attach it to the Google Calendar invite.

## Take notes, and have an agenda

I like to have a Google Doc I use with each of my 1-1s. You can see an example [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1ERxlDza1QDalb9hqrWUetraGGu_7ss7Q3camF0mz7bk/edit?usp=sharing).

I take notes during each 1-1, and add action items. The other person can as well. Either of us can add topics to the Backlog for future conversations.

## Format for first 1-1

For your first 1-1s with someone, I like to start with an outline like this:

-   <Greetings>
-   <If you don’t know them> Tell me a little about yourself, and I’ll do the same.
-   I see 1-1s as mostly your meeting. I'll sometimes have topics of my own as well, or have feedback to share with you. I like to keep notes in a shared Google doc, so we can keep a record of previous conversations. Feel free to use that Google doc like it's your agenda for our 1-1. Add topics you'd like to discuss.
-   Do you have any advice for me as your manager? (If they don’t respond to that, ask about the managers they’ve enjoyed working for, or not enjoyed working for, and to tell you what about it they enjoyed or didn’t. You’ll learn about their working style and get some hints on how to serve them well.)

Listen more than you talk.

## Format for 1-1s after the first one

-   <Greetings>
-   What is on your mind?
-   <Listen and discuss>
-   Then choose other questions from the "open ended questions list"

For the most part, you shouldn’t use your 1-1s for status. This means your status will need to come from other meetings or means.

## Open ended questions

Here are some open-ended questions you can ask:

-   What problems are you seeing nowadays?
-   How are you feeling about your work?
-   Can you think of something I can do to better support you?
-   How do you think everyone is doing on the team right now?
-   What are you wanting to get better at?
-   How do you think <last project or milestone> went? Anything we can learn from it?
-   How are these 1-1s going for you? Anything you'd like to change?

## Evolve to fit your style

You'll develop your own style of 1-1 as you get experience with it. That's fine -- everyone seems to do it differently. Just check in periodically to make sure you're being useful and helpful to your direct reports.
