Sometimes, everything falls apart. It can seem like everyone in the organization is about to quit.

I've seen this even at good companies. People have a crisis of confidence, and a bunch of people start quitting.

Your job as a leader is to make the situation better. Having lots of people leave can make that much harder. So when you're worried that people might quit, I recommend doing an exercise called a "retention review".

## Why a retention review

A retention review is a tool you can use. You employ it when you're worried about people leaving. When you use a retention review, you ensure you understand how everyone is doing. It highlights people you need to have conversations with. And it helps you address problems before they get worse.

## Do a retention review using this retention review template

A retention review is pretty easy to implement:

1.  Create a spreadsheet with everyone in your organization. You can use my [retention review template](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1lHGSVGnBGRaqwuqwE_dg1yIsL1OeNSw0oNLAUaPaMNw/edit#gid=0) and modify it. Let me know if you improve it significantly -- would love to see variations!
2.  Each manager fills it in for all of their team members.
3.  Identify team members you're not sure about.
4.  Also identify team members that might be unhappy or apathetic (yellow). And identify team members that are at risk of leaving (red).

## Identify action items

After filling out the retention review, identify action items. Choose items that are immediate and will either get you more information, or will help make the situation better.

Here are a few example action items:

1.  Move up 1-1 and ask how she's feeling about work. Dig in to details.
2.  In next 1-1, ask how onboarding into the job has been, and how they're feeling about all the change.
3.  Set up 1-1 this afternoon, ask what they'd like to see change. See if I can connect that to plans and changes in flight. Ask what would make them stay.

Maybe you can come up with better?

## After the retention review

The most important thing is that people need to see how things are going to get better. So you need to take stock of the situation, and identify the most important changes to make. And then you need to be real with people about the problems you face, and what you're doing to address it.

Importantly, you also need to take care of yourself. Times of turmoil are difficult. Sometimes they require you to push harder than usual. But be careful to manage your energy and mood so you can continue to be a support for others.
