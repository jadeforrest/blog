## Management requires context

Being successful as a manager requires context. You have to understand the people on your team, the state of projects, and how the team works.

## Learn context quickly with good handoff meetings

When you're new to a situation, you have to build that context as quickly as you can. This means you need to spend time digging into it. But you can accelerate how quickly you learn if you're able to do a handoff meeting with the outgoing manager.

What is a handoff meeting? It's a meeting where you try to learn as much as you can from the outgoing manager.

## How I do handoff meetings

I schedule three or four one hour meetings with the outgoing manager. Each has a focus. I ask questions like the following for each meeting.

### Handoff meeting: People

1.  Go over people on team. What are each person's strengths? Where are growth areas? Any performance issues? Take notes.
2.  For each person, is there anything that you think hasn't been well represented by their reviews?
3.  What reputation do people have on the team? Is that fair, or is there more to it?
4.  Are there any promises that have been made to people on the team I should be aware of? Discussions about promotions or salaries?
5.  Is there anybody on the team that is underleveled?
6.  Is there anyone on the team that is likely to leave, or is unhappy right now?
7.  What roles do different people take on the team? How do people work together?
8.  How well does the team understand what it owns?

### Handoff meeting: Projects

1.  Go over the current projects.
2.  What are current risks and concerns for current projects? What is likely to go wrong?
3.  Are there project plans, technical plans, or other artifacts you can look at?
4.  Go over stakeholders for the project (and in general). What do stakeholders care about. What information needs do they have?
5.  Is there work that needs to happen that isn't well represented in the roadmap?

### Handoff meeting: Process

1.  Go over team process. What are the team meetings? How are projects run?
2.  How are things decided on the team currently? What roles do you have?
3.  How are bugs triaged? Who does it?
4.  What's the oncall rotation like?
5.  How does the team work with product, design, support, and sales?
6.  Go over cross-functional relationships.
7.  Make sure you understand any outstanding issues.
8.  Anything I need to be careful to not mess up?

If I've scheduled a fourth meeting, I'll follow up on any questions that I've had from any of the other meetings.

You'll usually learn a great deal from these sessions, and that will supplement your own investigation into each of these topics on your own. I recommend doing deep dives into these topics as an individual as well. Read previous reviews, look over project artifacts, and read up on any documentation the team has about how they operate.

## Dangers with handoff meetings

One of the challenges with handoff meetings is that you can be biased by the previous manager. If they think a particular employee is problematic, you may or may not agree with them. But the handoff meeting has a risk of coloring your judgment.

I view the advantages as vastly outweighing those risks. But take care to not take the outgoing manager's perspective as the truth.
