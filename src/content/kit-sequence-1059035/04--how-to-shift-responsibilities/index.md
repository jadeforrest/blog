One of the most important skills for a manager is recognizing limits. You have to develop the ability to take things that are overwhelming or unrealistic, and chart out paths to make them achievable and realistic.

Almost all managers struggle with bumping into the limits of their capacity. Why? People will ask more and more of you until you stop being able to say yes to them.

There is more to this than just saying no. As you become more fluent as a leader, you'll start to develop a set of patterns you apply when you get new responsibilities. But what I commonly see people do is react to new responsibilities like it's a new situation, every single time.

Today we'll cover a couple of patterns I've found useful for **training people**, and **shifting responsibilities**. But first, let's talk about coding while managing.

## Coding and managing at the same time

One of the more common challenges I see new managers get into is coding and managing at the same time. While this isn't _always_ a bad idea, it can be more challenging than you might expect.

Some companies set up the engineering management role to still do technical work (usually at the expense of other parts of the job) -- usually your role is called "Tech Lead". And sometimes you're told to transition into the new role, while still doing the old one.

When I first became a manager, I was told that I was still responsible for finishing the engineering work on a large project I was on. What I found was that it wasn't a realistic expectation. The work required of me as a manager was much more time-intensive than I expected. It was hard to find the focus time to do any engineering work.

So if you're in this situation, I encourage you to increase your level of vigilance. Monitor how it is going. And if you're finding it unreasonable, bring it up early with your manager.

Unless you're a Tech Lead, I recommend you stay close to the team's work, but not be on the critical path for anything the team does. It may be comfortable to work in code. But you're switching to a new role, so you should focus on the parts that aren't comfortable.

## Primary/Secondary/Out

Let's talk about how to transition your responsibilities to others.

You'll frequently encounter situations as a manager where you have someone with a unique set of skills. They'll be the only person that knows how to work on a particular part of the codebase. Or they'll be the only person that knows how to answer questions in a particular domain. In this case, it might be you, who needs to transfer some of your responsibilities elsewhere, so you can focus on management.

The general pattern to use is:

1.  **Primary**: Switch someone else to Primary. Identify someone, or a group of people, who can field the questions instead of you. Talk with that person (or group of people) and get them to agree to be the new Primary person for that area. And then tell everyone else to talk them instead of you.
2.  **Secondary**: Make yourself the Secondary person. Whenever the Primary person isn't able to answer a question (or handle a situation), they can escalate to you. You'll tell them the answers, and they'll respond. As you answer questions, you might also document them, so the Secondary person can use the documentation next time, instead of coming to you. If anyone comes to you with questions that isn't the Primary person, send them to the Primary person first. At first, they'll be escalating all the time. But after a while, they'll start to learn the domain, and become knowledgeable enough to handle an increasing percent of requests.
3.  **Out**: After a period of time has passed where there haven't been any escalations, you'll know that the Primary person is handling the new area. You're now Out. You can say you're still available for questions, but you then can formally tell the Primary person they're the expert now.

This is a general technique. You'll use it frequently as a manager. It takes time to build up enough expertise that you can support an area of the company. And responsibilities and people move around. So keep this in your back pocket.

## See one, do one, teach one

A second pattern you can use for teaching people skills is "see one, do one, teach one."

This is a general technique you can use to master skills yourself, or have someone else master a skill. The idea is to:

1.  **See one**: watch someone do it. This helps you see what is involved and understand how it is done by others.
2.  **Do one**: do it yourself. Doing something requires a different level of competence than seeing something. Now you're forced to understand all the steps and skills required to do it yourself.
3.  **Teach one**: explain it to someone else. Explaining something requires you to understand it even better than if you do it. It makes everything explicit that was hidden. It forces you to analyze what you've done, and makes you able to summarize the approach.

So for example, if you're taking over the team meetings as a manager, you might:

1.  **See one**: for the first week, observe the outgoing manager as they run all of their team meetings.
2.  **Do one**: the next week, run the team meetings yourself.
3.  **Teach one**: the next week, write down how the team's meetings work and put them in the wiki.

## General tools

Both "Primary/Secondary/Out" and "See one, do one, teach one" are tools you can use in many situations:

-   With process
-   With meetings
-   With projects

They're enormously handy tools to have in your toolbox.

## Homework

Action: think about your current role and previous role. Are there any areas you need to hand off? Make a plan for how you'll hand off any of your old responsibilities.
