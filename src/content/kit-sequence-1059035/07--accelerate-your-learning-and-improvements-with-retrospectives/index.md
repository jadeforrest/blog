## What's the point of retrospectives?

Retrospectives are a common ritual for software teams. Yet, quality for retrospectives can vary. Retrospectives can be a source of learning and continuous improvement for a team. They can help you adapt your team, and learn what isn't working. This post outlines a few things you might want to think about as you run retrospectives as a manager or technical lead.

Note that incident retrospectives can require a different format. [This guide from Jeli](https://www.jeli.io/howie-the-post-incident-guide/) is a good place to start for how to follow up from incidents.

## What makes a good retrospective?

When I think about the best retrospectives I've been a part of, they contain these elements:

-   People feel safe bringing up hard topics.
-   They are action oriented but realistic.
-   The discussion is thoughtful and engaging.
-   The team celebrates what went well together.
-   I leave the meeting feeling like I learned something.

So how do you create retrospectives that feel like that?

## Tip: observe the retrospective, not just the retrospective content

You can learn as much from a retro by observation as from the content of the retro. Some things to watch out for:

-   Who talks and who does?
-   Who contributes? Does anyone seem uncomfortable bringing up hard topics?
-   What happens when someone does bring up something uncomfortable? Is it welcomed, or shut down?
-   Whose ideas get traction, and who gets shut down?

Watching these things can give you your own personal "retro action items" to follow up on. They can give you signals to where your team isn't really acting as a team.

## Tip: try a shared doc to collect feedback quickly

You can use a shared document to collect feedback quickly. This can be a good approach because it encourages broad participation in the retrospective. You may get feedback from people who might not otherwise speak up.

It's also more efficient, because everyone can enter their feedback and comments at the same time.

One thing you might try is to create the retro doc for the NEXT retro right after the current retro is complete. Some people like to prepare for things ahead of time, and don't think as quickly in the moment. Giving them a place to note down their thoughts might result in better participation.

You can even distribute it right after the last retro, so people who want to can add stuff between retros.

## Tip: try this format for retrospectives

I've written up a potential way to run a retrospective. You can use this [template](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1nGJcW2uXbJFDA2XFXxmNkjXXHbsKl3offRVHYrjWe94/edit?usp=sharing). It includes:

-   An agenda for the meeting
-   Questions that act as prompts
-   A method to quickly collect ideas.
-   A method to identify topics to spend the rest of the time in discussion.
-   A method to identify action items and assign them.
-   A method to identify things you've learned during the incident.

There are a lot of ways to run a retrospective, so you might adapt it over time. Let me know what changes you make!

Here's how the meeting is typically run

1.  You start out by reminding people of why we do retrospectives. We want to learn and be a better team. And we want to identify actions or changes we can make to help us adapt and work better together.
2.  Set a timer, and say we'll spend 5 minutes adding bullet points for thoughts about the last week. You can enter kudos and things that went well in the first section. And enter observations, concerns, and problems you've noticed in the second section.
3.  Ask people to put their cursor in the "cursor parking lot" when they're done. If you see all the cursors are done, you can move on to the next topic. Otherwise, remind people at the 5 minute mark. You can extend it another 5 minutes if people are still actively writing.
4.  Use indentation for threading. People can reply to each other's comments, or add ideas for improvements.
5.  Next, tell the team we'll spend a few minutes "dot voting". Each person gets 5 votes per section. They can put their name at the end of the line to indicate they are voting to talk about this topic. You can put multiple votes in a line.
6.  After people have voted, sort the list by number of votes. You now have a sorted list of topics to discuss as a team.
7.  Go through each topic and discuss it. For each section, give people time to talk about it. The goal is to identify "learnings" and "action items".
8.  By default, action items should be completed within the next week. Be sure not to take on too many action items. If it looks like there are too many to be reasonable, talk about which are most important, and strike the rest. They'll come up again if they're important.

## How often should you have retrospectives?

I like to have a retrospective after every milestone in a project, ideally every couple of weeks. But you should schedule additional retros whenever you think you can learn from something.

You shouldn't be having more retros than your capacity to do the action items from retros.

## Midtrospectives and Speculaspectives

Keep in mind you don't only have to have retrospectives.

You can do "midtrospectives" in the middle of a project. A midtrospective might focus on "what's going well" and "what are we worrying about or think might not go well".

You can even do Speculaspectives. Ask "what do we think will go well with this project". And "If something were to go wrong in this project, what do we think it might be?" This can be an effect way to identify risks, and help ensure a critical project is successful.

## Tinker with the format

When you're first putting in place retrospectives, reserve a few minutes at the end of each retro and ask people if they have any suggestions for improving the next retro. See what people say. You don't have to do their suggestion, but you can experiment continually until you feel like it's one of the most valuable meetings of your week.
