As a manager, you'll often encounter the situation where you have a member of your team that everyone is saying terrible things about. That's tricky enough to navigate -- but it gets really difficult when you don't know the team member's work very well.

You may feel pressure to fire them. The people who are telling you these things usually have an opinion of what the issue is. And they'll sometimes be Very Important People, like CEOs, founders, or VPs.

## You must quickly build context

Having been in this situation many times, I've found that the perceptions that float around people doesn't always match reality.

So here's what I usually end up doing: the **individual deep dive**. Your goal is to become more knowledgeable about the situation than anyone. And fast.

Realistically, you may not reach that goal, but get as close to it as you can. Here's how.

## Example situation: George's feedback on Beth

George came to me frustrated. He said, "I don't even know why Beth is working here. She's coasting, producing less code than anyone else on your team."

Beth hadn't been on the team long, so I didn't know her work very well. I did know George, however. He was a founding engineer at the company, and was currently working with my team on their current project. He had a reputation of being smart but difficult to work with. I knew that George had had some disagreements with Beth in design meetings. George was ornery, but it was hard to argue with his track record. His technical judgement was quite good -- in retrospect, his recommendations always seemed like they were the right direction, even when they seemed bizarre or counter-intuitive.

My short time with Beth on the team was actually pretty favorable. She seemed experienced and smart. After George, the most experienced person on the team.

This wasn't the first time I had heard this feedback on Beth, however. When Beth had transferred from another team, the outgoing manager had said something about her not being very productive, but contributing in other ways. When she was moved to my team, my manager said to watch her work closely. He didn't really have any details to offer, so this was the sort of ugly rumors that swirl around people sometimes.

What was strange is she seemed very present in team meetings, and it seemed like she was actively contributing. I just didn't see many pull requests in her name.

## First, orient thyself

When I face an issue like this, I like to reflect on the situation. I'm the type of person that needs space to think through things. So the way I do it is to sit down and interrogate myself through writing. Here's how I do it -- you may find your own alternative to this. I write down questions and thoughts like this:

-   Is the core issue that Beth is not (or appears to not be) productive? Or is this a relationship issue between George and Beth?
-   What evidence am I seeing about productivity? PR requests, or something else? Is that a real thing, or is it the appearance of a problem. For example, could she be pairing a lot? Is she prioritizing other valuable work on the team? Is she doing a lot of [glue work](https://noidea.dog/glue)?
-   What's going on between George and Beth? I missed the meeting but heard about the conflict they had. George has a lot of influence, and I'm curious if he's spreading the word about Beth more widely than to me, her manager. If so, I need to have a talk with him.
-   What are the perceptions of the rest of the team about Beth? What are their perceptions about how George is interacting with the team?
-   Has anybody given feedback on these points to Beth before? What kind of history exists here? Has anybody given feedback to George on how he's interacting with Beth? Curious if he's done this sort of thing in the past with other people. I seem to remember something like this before, again with the most senior person on the team having a conflict with George when he was working in the team's area.
-   What does a good outcome look like here? Seems like I need to investigate how George and Beth are interacting, need to understand Beth's history better, and need to understand whether George has had these type of interactions with others in the past. And need to dig in to how the team and previous managers have seen the situation. I also need to evaluate whether Beth is performing well, whether there is a perception issue that needs to be addressed. Whew.
-   Anything to be careful of? I'm curious if Beth is being put in a bad situation. Also feel like the power dynamics here are uneven. Since I've heard similar feedback from several sources, I need to figure out if the feedback is real, or if George is spreading this widely. If so, George is being both unprofessional and harming Beth's career, which needs serious intervention. But if Beth is really not pulling her weight, that also requires intervention and could result in her needing to leave the company. Spicy.

## Exercise: the individual deep dive

The orientation you've done is good ground work for the individual deep dive. Now let's get started.

1.  **Create a list of individuals** who can help you understand the situation better. I came up with: all present team members, a few previous coworkers of Beth, George's manager, George, Beth, and Beth's previous manager.
2.  **Set up 1-1s with everyone**. This is painful, but try to have them all happen within the next couple of days. Consider deferring your other meetings, so you can get to the bottom of this quickly.
3.  **Prep a list of questions you'll use in your 1-1s**.
4.  **Take notes during the 1-1s**.
5.  **Draw conclusions and create action items** when you're done.

I use these interviews, and supplement them with any other information sources I might have: reviews, any tools we use that can give me more insight, and meeting notes.

## Questions for exploratory 1-1s

Doing these type of 1-1s can be fraught. You might be telegraphing that a situation is happening. And if you end up having to let someone go after such an investigation, you're creating a scary signal when you do future exploratory 1-1s like this.

But the truth of the matter is that you're trying to help make this situation better. You want to give good feedback to George and to Beth to help them be more impactful. So you have to balance your need to get this information, with your need to not reveal too much about it.

What I've generally done is made a habit of asking team members, and people I work with, about how the team is doing. I bring up observations of things that seemed difficult from meetings, and ask people how they thought about how it went. When you've built up trust within your team, and they see that you're actively trying to help grow the people within the team, that will make them more likely to want to participate in questions about how their teammates are doing.

I generally favor open-ended questions. This can show if things are on people's minds. But I try not to lead the discussion too much. For this situation, it might be natural to ask how the meeting went where there was conflict between George and Beth: "I heard there was some conflict between George and Beth at the planning meeting last week. I wasn't able to be there, do you mind telling me about it?" And then ask some followup questions about how George and Beth are interacting with the team.

Other questions I sometimes will ask are "do you have any advice for George when he's working with teams like ours?". Or, "do you have any suggestions for Beth that you think would help her be more impactful or effective?"

Before my 1-1 with Beth, I would talk with some team members. I might find that many of them talk about her pairing with them, and that might alleviate potential concerns about productivity. Or I might find she's committing to another codebase. I have to do that homework ahead of time. Because otherwise, I'm making her defend herself from something that may not even be real.

## Bias happens during these interviews too

It's important to realize no team member has the truth about someone. And you may uncover other biases or slanted viewpoints.

However, after interviewing a bunch of people, you'll naturally have a much more nuanced view of the situation.

## Decide and then act

Once you've made your conclusions, and come up with action items, act on them quickly.

In this situation, I found that most of the critique of Beth wasn't grounded in fact: she was doing a lot of important work for the team, but it wasn't represented by the number of PRs that were made in her name. She was pairing more than anyone on the team, and had a habit of having the less experienced team members do the PRs. But she did need to learn to work more effectively with George. So I coached her on that.

After talking with George's manager, we agreed that it would be best for me to talk directly with George. I found that George was talking more broadly about his concerns with Beth, so I had to have a firm conversation with him about that. I told him I had looked into her work, and gave evidence she was productive and contributing to the team. I told him he should make it right for her by correcting the misinformation he had spread. And I also talked him about the challenges of working with teams that he isn't a part of, and suggested some ways to handle that better.

## Was it worth doing the deep dive?

Without doing the deeper investigation, I might have gone directly to Beth and shared the critique that she wasn't being productive. That would have made her felt defensive and misunderstood. If I wasn't careful, she could have ended up getting fired, or leaving of her own accord.

I also could have had my opinion about her productivity be shaped by George, and had it affect how I interact with Beth.

## When is it not worth it?

I only recommend doing a deep dive when there is a serious situation, and you haven't been working with someone long enough to have a good working understanding of that person.

(By the way, the situation with George and Beth is a fictional scenario, but a blend of many real situations I've been in)
